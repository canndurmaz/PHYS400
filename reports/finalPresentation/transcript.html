2 Why Two Elastic Constants Govern Structural Design ~70 s · ~160 words
The selection of a structural alloy is principally governed by its elastic response, summarised by two constants: Young's modulus E — the stiffness — and Poisson's ratio ν. The table on the right shows where these decide the design outcome: aerospace structures, turbine components, biomedical implants, automotive structures, civil engineering, and precision electromechanical devices. Between them, these two numbers set beam deflection, buckling load, natural frequency, the bulk modulus through K = E / 3(1 − 2ν), stress concentration, and fracture toughness. (pause) For a cubic crystal, symmetry collapses the stress–strain law σ = Cε — shown as the six-by-six matrix — to just three independent constants, C11, C12, and C44; Young's modulus and Poisson's ratio follow from C11 and C12 — the quantities this work targets from first principles — subject to the Born–Cauchy stability criterion C11 > C12, which recurs as the principal verification gate in Stage 5b. Measuring these constants experimentally is costly; atomistic simulation is far cheaper — provided an accurate interatomic potential exists for the alloy. That condition motivates the whole framework.
Procedure: Reference the table by gesture; do not enumerate rows. State the Born–Cauchy inequality explicitly — it is the closing verification gate.
Background — the cubic elastic constants

When you pull on a solid it stretches, and springs back when released. Stress σ is force per unit area; strain ε is the fractional stretch. For small deformations they are proportional — Hooke's law F = kx generalised to three dimensions: σ = Cε, where C is the stiffness tensor, the material's collection of “spring constants.” It is not a single number because pushing or shearing in different directions gives different responses.

Stress and strain each have six independent components (three normal, three shear), so C is a 6×6 matrix — in full generality 21 independent numbers. Crystal symmetry is the great simplifier: a cubic crystal has a cube-shaped cell with three identical axes, and that symmetry forces most entries to be equal or zero, leaving exactly three independent constants — C₁₁, C₁₂, C₄₄:

σ = Cε,  C = 
⎡ C₁₁ C₁₂ C₁₂  0   0   0 ⎤
⎢ C₁₂ C₁₁ C₁₂  0   0   0 ⎥
⎢ C₁₂ C₁₂ C₁₁  0   0   0 ⎥
⎢  0   0   0  C₄₄  0   0 ⎥
⎢  0   0   0   0  C₄₄  0 ⎥
⎣  0   0   0   0   0  C₄₄⎦
  • C₁₁ — stiffness against stretching along a cube edge: how hard the bonds resist being pulled apart.
  • C₁₂ — the sideways coupling: squeeze along x and the crystal pushes out in y and z. This is the origin of Poisson's ratio.
  • C₄₄ — resistance to shear (sliding atomic planes past each other); genuinely independent of the other two in a cubic crystal. Its departure from (C₁₁−C₁₂)/2 measures the crystal's anisotropy.

The engineering quantities follow directly: bulk modulus K = (C₁₁+2C₁₂)/3, and along [100] E = (C₁₁−C₁₂)(C₁₁+2C₁₂)/(C₁₁+C₁₂), ν = C₁₂/(C₁₁+C₁₂). So C₁₁ and C₁₂ together fix E and ν — exactly the two numbers this framework targets; C₄₄ governs shear, which is not the target.

Why C₁₁ > C₁₂ is the stability gate. A crystal only holds together if its strain energy is positive for any deformation (Born stability), which for cubic means C₁₁−C₁₂ > 0, C₁₁+2C₁₂ > 0, and C₄₄ > 0. The inequality on the slide is the first of these. A potential predicting C₁₂ ≥ C₁₁ describes a crystal that would spontaneously shear apart — physically meaningless — which is precisely the gate where 19 of 30 alloys fail in Stage 5b.

3 Atomistic Modelling — Interatomic Potentials and DFT ~60 s · ~140 words
Before the method, two terms for the jury. An interatomic potential is simply a fast, approximate formula for the energy — and hence the forces — between atoms; MEAM is one such formula, physically tuned for metals. Density-functional theory, or DFT, is the slow but accurate quantum-mechanical alternative we trust as the reference. The whole framework is about making the fast formula reproduce the accurate one. (pause) More precisely, an interatomic potential is a closed-form approximation of the many-body potential energy of a system of atoms; once specified, it yields forces by differentiation and permits classical molecular-dynamics simulation at scales inaccessible to first-principles methods. The historical development proceeds from pairwise potentials — inadequate for metallic bonding — through the Embedded Atom Method of Daw and Baskes, to the Modified EAM, which introduces angular partial densities and constitutes the established formalism for metallic systems of mixed bonding character. (pause) Density-functional theory provides the complementary first-principles route: a quantum-mechanical solver from which cohesive energies, lattice constants, bulk moduli, and elastic constants are obtained directly. DFT is of high accuracy but is limited to systems of order one thousand atoms; MEAM is empirical but accommodates six orders of magnitude more. The present work uses DFT as the reference against which the MEAM potential is calibrated.
Procedure: Underline the accuracy/scale trade-off explicitly — it is the rationale for the entire pipeline.
Background — potentials vs. first principles

Every property of a material follows from its energy as a function of where the atoms sit, U(r). The force on an atom is just the downhill slope of that energy, F = −∂U/∂r. An interatomic potential is a cheap closed-form guess for U; molecular dynamics simply lets the atoms roll downhill on it.

The simplest guess — sum over pairs of atoms (Lennard–Jones) — works for noble gases but fails for metals, where a shared “sea” of electrons means an atom's energy depends on how many neighbours surround it, not just pair distances. EAM fixes this with an embedding energy (the cost of placing an atom in the local electron density); MEAM adds angular terms so it can distinguish BCC, HCP, and directional bonding.

DFT skips the guessing: it approximately solves the quantum equations for the electrons themselves, predicting U from first principles — but costs so much it caps at ~10³ atoms, versus ~10⁶+ for MEAM. So DFT is the accurate ruler; MEAM is the fast tool calibrated against it. That trade-off is the whole reason this pipeline exists.

4 The MEAM Coverage Problem ~55 s · ~125 words
The principal obstacle addressed in this work is the fragmented coverage of the published MEAM literature. The table summarises four representative libraries; each is limited to a small element subset, and no single file spans the nine elements of the Al 7075 specification. (pause) Direct merging is not admissible: the cross-interaction parameters between elements drawn from distinct libraries are undefined, and manual refitting does not scale — twelve elements give sixty-six binary pairs. The contemporary alternative — purely data-driven machine-learning potentials of the Behler–Parrinello, GAP, or MTP families — avoids the coverage problem but requires between ten thousand and one hundred thousand DFT configurations and forfeits the physical interpretability of MEAM. The present framework therefore retains the MEAM formalism and constructs the missing parameters automatically.
Procedure: Reference the table once by gesture; the “merged” row is the visual answer to the problem.
Background — why you can't just merge two MEAM files

A MEAM library is a text file of fitted parameters: per-element functions plus pair-specific cross-terms describing how each pair of elements interacts. Published files were each fitted for a particular handful of elements together.

You cannot concatenate two files, because the cross-terms between an element from file A and one from file B were never fitted — they simply do not exist. With twelve elements there are C(12,2) = 66 such pairs to define, far too many to hand-tune. The modern alternative — purely data-driven machine-learning potentials — avoids the problem but needs 10⁴–10⁵ DFT snapshots and becomes an uninterpretable black box. Keeping MEAM preserves physical meaning and a tiny parameter count; this work just fills in the missing cross-terms automatically.

5 Proposed Methodology — Five-Stage Pipeline ~75 s · ~170 words
The proposed methodology comprises five sequential stages. Stage 1 generates random alloy compositions on the twelve-element simplex. Stage 2 evaluates each composition by LAMMPS molecular dynamics, yielding the cubic stiffness constants C11 and C12 for the entire corpus. Stage 3 performs Quantum-Espresso DFT calculations for each element and each binary pair, producing first-principles cohesive energies, lattice constants, bulk moduli, and formation energies. (pause) Stage 4 maps these reference values onto the MEAM parameter space, yielding an initialised twelve-element library that includes the previously undefined cross-terms. Stage 5 closes the loop: a neural-network surrogate of the MEAM-parameters-to-stiffness map is inverse-optimised, under L-BFGS, against the Stage-2 targets. The dashed branch denotes Stage 5a, an auxiliary surrogate that maps composition directly to the stiffness constants and is distributed as an interactive predictor independent of the MEAM-refitting loop.
Procedure: The longest segment of the talk. Indicate each stage with the laser pointer in turn; do not return to earlier stages.
Background — the inverse-problem trick

The core idea is solving an inverse problem. The forward direction is easy: given MEAM parameters, LAMMPS computes the elastic constants. We want the reverse — the parameters that produce a target set of constants — which has no formula.

So we train a neural network to imitate the forward map (parameters → C₁₁, C₁₂). Because a neural network is differentiable, we can ask “which way should I nudge the parameters to reduce the error?” and let an optimiser (L-BFGS, a gradient-based method) walk downhill to a solution — vastly faster than blindly searching a ~200-dimensional parameter space. Stage 5a is a separate, simpler net that predicts properties straight from composition.

Background — the composition simplex

A composition is a list of element fractions that must sum to one; the set of all such lists is called the simplex. Sampling it uniformly gives an even spread of candidate alloys. We deliberately bias toward aluminium because the engineering target (Al 7075) is Al-rich — while still sampling the other eleven elements often enough to identify their cross-terms (you cannot fit an interaction you never observe).

7 Stage 2 — Molecular-Dynamics Elastic Database ~60 s · ~135 words
Stage 2 evaluates each configuration in LAMMPS. A custom build of the code was required: the upstream distribution caps the number of MEAM species at five, whereas the merged library produced in Stage 4 contains twelve. The patched build raises the limit to twenty. (pause) For each configuration, a cubic supercell of approximately four nanometres is constructed from the lattice constant of the dominant element; atom types are then reassigned to the target mass fractions and the cell is minimised under anisotropic strain. A small axial strain of magnitude ten-to-the-minus-three is applied successively along each Cartesian direction, and the resulting stresses are central-differenced to recover C11 and C12. The scatter on the right shows that Mg- and Zn-rich compositions cluster near ν approaching one-half, which motivates training the surrogate on the stiffness constants rather than directly on (E, ν).
Procedure: The Mg/Zn cluster justifies the choice of training targets — state it explicitly.
Background — measuring stiffness by simulation

To measure stiffness in silico, you build a box of a few thousand atoms, relax it to its lowest-energy shape, then stretch it by a tiny known amount (here 0.1 %) and read off the stress that develops. Stress divided by strain, in each direction, gives C₁₁ and C₁₂ — the numerical (finite-difference) version of their definition.

Magnesium- and zinc-rich alloys come out with ν near 0.5 — nearly incompressible, like rubber — where E and ν become numerically explosive (the 1−2ν term in the denominator approaches zero). Training the surrogate on the well-behaved C₁₁, C₁₂ and converting afterwards avoids that instability.

8 Stage 3–4 — DFT References → MEAM Initialisation ~70 s · ~155 words
Stage 3 is the first-principles reference layer. Quantum Espresso 7.5, driven through the Atomic Simulation Environment, runs all self-consistent calculations, equation-of-state scans, and elastic-constant evaluations — plane-wave cut-off forty Rydberg, Marzari–Vanderbilt smearing, a six-cubed Monkhorst–Pack mesh, with spin polarisation for iron, chromium, and manganese. For each element a seven-point equation-of-state scan, fitted with Birch–Murnaghan, yields the lattice constant, cohesive energy, and bulk modulus; for each binary pair a single calculation gives the formation energy. The heat map summarises sixty-six pairs, whose signed values seed the MEAM cross-terms directly. (pause) Stage 4 then closes the formal gap: the MEAM screening exponent α follows analytically from each element's DFT triple, by the expression in the box. Together with the seeded cross-terms this gives a complete twelve-element library in about seventy milliseconds. Only the α-manganese equation of state failed to converge; a relaxed body-centred-cubic approximation stands in.
Procedure: Seeding the cross-terms directly is the hand-off from DFT to MEAM. Do not derive α on the board — cite its MEAM origin.
Background — what DFT measures, and how it seeds MEAM

For each element, DFT computes the energy at several lattice spacings; fitting that energy-versus-volume curve (the Birch–Murnaghan equation of state) yields three numbers: the equilibrium spacing a₀, the cohesive energy (how strongly an atom is bound), and the bulk modulus B (resistance to uniform compression). For each pair of elements, the formation energy says whether mixing is favourable (negative — the elements like to alloy) or not (positive — they tend to separate).

Stage 4 then converts each element's three DFT numbers into the MEAM screening parameter α through a closed-form formula — no fitting required — while the pair formation energies seed the cross-terms directly. The result is a complete twelve-element starting library in milliseconds.

9 Stage 5a — Composition Surrogate ~55 s · ~125 words
Stage 5a is an auxiliary branch of the pipeline: a small neural network that consumes the LAMMPS elastic database directly and does not feed back into the MEAM refitting. It serves two functions — a consistency check on the molecular-dynamics output, and a fast interactive predictor of E and ν from an arbitrary composition vector. (pause) The architecture is a fully-connected network with layers of twelve, thirty-two, twenty, and two units, trained under Huber loss on C11 and C12, with a five-seed deep ensemble for uncertainty quantification. On the thirty-percent held-out split, the coefficient of determination for E is 0.77 and for ν is 0.52. On the Al 7075 preset the surrogate returns 75.5 gigapascals and a Poisson's ratio of 0.334, consistent with the published reference range.
Procedure: Reference the deployed application image; do not propose a live demonstration.
Background — reading the surrogate's metrics

A small neural network learns the map composition → (C₁₁, C₁₂). The R² score rates the fit: 1.0 is perfect, 0 is no better than always guessing the average. R² = 0.77 on E is solid; 0.52 on ν reflects how intrinsically noisy ν is. Huber loss is a hybrid error measure — quadratic for small errors, only linear for large ones — so a few wild outlier alloys don't dominate training.

Running five independently-initialised networks (a deep ensemble) and watching how much they disagree gives a cheap, honest uncertainty estimate — useful for flagging compositions far from the training data.

10 Stage 5b — NN-Driven MEAM Parameter Optimisation ~65 s · ~150 words
Stage 5b constitutes the closed-loop core of the framework. A differentiable neural-network surrogate is trained to map the MEAM parameter vector onto the stiffness constants, with training data drawn from either Sobol or active-learning samples evaluated in LAMMPS. The L-BFGS optimiser minimises the Huber loss through the surrogate; the optimised parameters are subsequently re-evaluated in LAMMPS, new samples are appended, and the surrogate is retrained. Atomic checkpointing renders the procedure resumable. (pause) The surrogate Huber loss decays from approximately unity to a residual value of 0.16 over ten thousand training steps. Verification on thirty held-out alloys reveals the current limitations: of these thirty, only eleven satisfy the Born–Cauchy stability criterion; among those eleven, four lie within the twenty-percent engineering tolerance on both E and ν. The NN-optimised MEAM therefore does not yet constitute a deployable potential on the present twelve-element corpus.
Procedure: State the verification numbers without qualifying language; the closing slide addresses the interpretation.
Background — why the loop is hard

This is the same surrogate idea as Stage 5a, but now over MEAM parameters instead of composition, run as a loop: optimise → re-check the result in LAMMPS → append the new data → retrain. Active learning means the loop chooses its next samples where the model is least certain, rather than at random.

The difficulty is that the problem is under-determined — roughly 200 free parameters but at most ~100 elastic targets — so many different parameter sets fit the data equally well, and most describe mechanically unstable crystals. The Born–Cauchy check (C₁₁ > C₁₂, explained on Slide 2) is the filter: only 11 of 30 held-out alloys survive it, and 4 then meet the 20 % engineering tolerance.

11 External Validation — Quantitative Summary ~40 s · ~95 words
To validate both surrogates against independent experimental data, I scraped Young's modulus and Poisson's ratio for forty-three engineering alloys from the ASM Aerospace materials database and fed each measured composition through both models. Across the thirty-three aluminium alloys, the composition network predicts Young's modulus to a mean absolute error of two-point-three percent, the MEAM to seven-point-four percent — and that agreement holds uniformly across the two-, five-, six- and seven-thousand series. (pause) Off the aluminium domain the error climbs steeply for the MEAM — its cubic-strain assumption fails on hexagonal titanium and ferrous chemistries, some even mechanically unstable — and far more gently for the composition network.

Background — what is compared, and how to read it

Datasheets give weight-percent compositions and polycrystalline E and ν; the models consume mole fractions and emit single-crystal cubic C₁₁, C₁₂. The script converts weight to mole fractions over the twelve-element basis — dropping out-of-basis elements such as carbon in steel and vanadium in Ti–6Al–4V — and compares the derived E and ν.

MAPE is the mean absolute percent error against the datasheet value. For the MEAM columns, mechanically unstable runs — negative modulus, or failing the Born–Cauchy check from Slide 2 — are excluded rather than allowed to dominate; the A-286 MEAM cell is blank for exactly this reason. The breakdown by aluminium alloy series confirms the agreement is a property of the whole family, not an artefact of one composition.

12 Further Studies ~60 s · ~140 words
The architecture proposed here is complete; the further studies fall into three groups. First, making the optimisation well-posed: ranking the MEAM cross-terms by Sobol sensitivity so that only the influential few are fitted, baking the Born–Cauchy stability inequalities directly into the loss, and expanding the active-learning corpus to at least five hundred composition medoids. (pause) Second — and this is what the external validation motivates — moving beyond the aluminium domain: extending the element basis to carbon, vanadium and nitrogen so that steels and titanium alloys become representable, broadening the molecular-dynamics and DFT sampling into the hexagonal and ferrous regions, and replacing the cubic-only elastic extraction with a symmetry-aware tensor. Third, the reference data: special-quasirandom-structure DFT at intermediate compositions, and a standing re-validation loop against the ASM experimental data. The remaining work is one of sampling, loss formulation, and domain coverage.
Procedure: Group the points by the three column headers; close on the final sentence, then acknowledge the jury and yield the floor for questions.
Background — the proposed directions, in plain terms

The first group attacks the under-determination from Slide 10. Sobol sensitivity analysis ranks which parameters actually move the elastic constants, so optimisation can be restricted to the few that matter (fewer unknowns → a better-posed problem); a physics-informed loss bakes the stability inequalities into training, penalising unstable solutions before they are proposed rather than catching them at the verification gate.

The second group is the lesson of the validation slides: both models were accurate only on aluminium because that is what they were built from. Adding the missing elements (carbon, vanadium…) and sampling hcp and ferrous chemistries widens the domain; a symmetry-aware elastic calculation (a hexagonal stiffness tensor for titanium, not the cubic one) removes the specific error that made MEAM titanium come out far too stiff. SQS — special quasirandom structures — are small cells engineered to mimic a random alloy, letting DFT supply references at intermediate compositions.

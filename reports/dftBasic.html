1 The problem DFT solves

We want to predict properties of materials — the lattice constant of aluminium, the cohesive energy of nickel, the elastic stiffness of a copper alloy — from first principles. That means: starting only from the identities of the atoms (their nuclear charges) and the laws of quantum mechanics, with no empirical fitting to experimental data.

Why first principles? Because once you have a method that works without experimental input, you can predict properties of materials that have never been synthesised, or are dangerous, or rare. That's the whole appeal of computational materials science.

2 Why the wavefunction is hopeless

In principle, everything about a material is contained in the many-body wavefunction — a single mathematical object that tells you the joint probability of finding every electron at every position simultaneously. Solve the Schrödinger equation for it, and you're done.

The catch: a wavefunction for N electrons lives in 3N-dimensional space. For just 100 electrons that's a function of 300 variables — storing it on any conceivable grid would require more atoms than exist in the observable universe. Direct attack is impossible for anything larger than a few atoms.

The wall: the curse of dimensionality. Every additional electron multiplies the problem size by another spatial dimension.
3 The Hohenberg–Kohn insight (1964)

Hohenberg and Kohn proved something startling: for the ground state of any electronic system, you don't actually need the full wavefunction. The electron density — just a real-valued function of three spatial coordinates, telling you how many electrons are at each point on average — uniquely determines everything: the total energy, the forces on every atom, the magnetic structure, all of it.

This is a dramatic reduction. Instead of a function of 3N variables, you only need to find the right function of 3 variables. The bookkeeping problem becomes tractable.

Conceptual leap: the density is far less information than the wavefunction — yet for ground-state properties, it's enough. That's the foundational claim of DFT.
4 The Kohn–Sham trick

Hohenberg–Kohn says "the density is enough" but doesn't tell you how to find it. Real electrons are entangled with each other, so even reformulating the problem in terms of density still leaves a knotted many-body interaction to deal with.

Kohn and Sham (1965) sidestep this with a clever bait-and-switch. They replace the real, interacting electrons with a fictitious system of non-interacting electrons, chosen so that this pretend system produces exactly the same density as the real one. Because the fictitious electrons don't interact, each one obeys a much simpler single-particle Schrödinger-like equation. Stack N of those, solve them, and you recover the density of the original interacting problem.

All the messy many-body physics — quantum exchange, electron correlation, all the parts where electrons "feel" each other beyond simple electrostatic repulsion — gets swept into a single extra term added to the effective potential. That term is the exchange–correlation functional, and it's where the entire approximation lives.

5 The exchange–correlation functional

If we knew the exact exchange–correlation (XC) functional, DFT would be exact. We don't. The community has built increasingly sophisticated approximations, organised into a rough hierarchy:

  • LDA (Local Density Approximation) — at each point in space, treat the electron gas as if it were locally uniform. Crude but surprisingly useful; the simplest rung.
  • GGA (Generalised Gradient Approximation) — also look at how fast the density is changing. PBE, the most widely used GGA, is the workhorse for solids and what we use here.
  • meta-GGA, hybrids, and beyond — mix in kinetic-energy information or a fraction of exact Hartree–Fock exchange. More accurate, much more expensive, sometimes overkill for the question you're asking.

Choice of functional is a modelling decision, not a settled fact. Different functionals can give somewhat different lattice constants or formation energies for the same material. You pick one that's known to perform well for your class of system — for metallic alloys, PBE is a defensible default.

Why this matters: when people say "DFT gives the wrong answer," they almost always mean "this particular XC functional gives the wrong answer." DFT as a framework is exact in principle.
6 Pseudopotentials — why we ignore the core

An aluminium atom has 13 electrons. Most of them sit deep in the core, tightly bound to the nucleus, and play almost no role in chemistry or bonding — they're chemically inert. Only the outer valence electrons participate in forming bonds.

Simulating the core electrons explicitly would force us to resolve very sharp wiggles in the wavefunction near the nucleus, which is computationally brutal. So we cheat: we replace the nucleus-plus-core bundle with a smooth, effective pseudopotential that the valence electrons feel exactly as if the real thing were there. The valence electrons see the correct chemistry; the messy core wiggles are gone.

Pseudopotentials come in flavours (PAW, ultrasoft, norm-conserving) with different trade-offs between accuracy and cost. Each element needs one, and they're calibrated separately — you can't mix and match carelessly.

In this project: we use PAW (Projector Augmented Wave) PBE pseudopotentials from the QE library. The NNIP pipeline auto-downloads any missing ones.
7 Crystals, periodicity, and k-points

A real crystal is enormous — Avogadro-scale. We obviously can't simulate that many atoms. But a perfect crystal is also boringly repetitive: one small repeating block (the unit cell) tiles space forever. We exploit this with periodic boundary conditions — simulate one unit cell and pretend it's surrounded by identical copies of itself in every direction.

For periodic systems, the natural language is not real space but reciprocal space: each electronic state is labelled by a wavevector k that captures its phase pattern across unit cells. To compute anything physically meaningful, we have to sum over many such wavevectors — this is the k-point sampling. More k-points means a finer sampling and more accurate energies, but more compute.

For metals there's an additional subtlety: the boundary between occupied and unoccupied states (the Fermi surface) is a sharp cliff in reciprocal space, and sampling near a cliff is numerically unstable. We smear that cliff slightly — just enough to converge cleanly without distorting the answer. This is the "smearing" you see in QE input files.

8 The self-consistent loop

The Kohn–Sham equations have a chicken-and-egg structure: the effective potential each electron feels depends on the density, which depends on solving the equations, which depends on the potential.

The standard fix is iteration. Start with a guess for the density. Build the potential from that guess. Solve the single-particle equations to get new wavefunctions and a new density. Mix the new density with the old to avoid oscillation. Repeat until the density stops changing meaningfully from one iteration to the next. This is the SCF loop (self-consistent field). When SCF has converged, you have a consistent ground state.

Practical note: "SCF didn't converge" is one of the most common DFT failure modes. Usually it means smearing is too sharp, the system is metallic and unstable, or the initial guess is too far off.
9 What DFT actually gives you

Once SCF converges, the immediate outputs are the total energy of the system, the forces on every atom (how much each atom wants to move), and the stress tensor on the simulation cell (how much the box wants to expand, contract, or shear).

From those three primitives, almost everything physically interesting follows:

  • Equilibrium lattice constant — vary the box size, find the volume that minimises the energy.
  • Cohesive energy — compare the energy of the crystal to isolated atoms; how much energy holds the solid together.
  • Bulk modulus & equation of state — how stiff the material is under uniform compression.
  • Elastic constants — apply small strains, read off the resulting stresses, get the full stiffness tensor.
  • Formation energies of alloys and compounds — compare the energy of a binary phase to its constituent elements; tells you whether the phase prefers to exist.
  • Phonons, vacancy energies, surface energies, reaction barriers — all derived from the same underlying energies and forces.
In this project: DFT provides the reference data (a₀, cohesive energy, bulk modulus, C₁₁ / C₁₂, binary formation energies) that initialises the MEAM interatomic potentials before NN refinement.
10 Where DFT struggles

DFT is not magic; it has well-known limitations worth knowing before you trust a result.

  • Ground state only. Standard DFT describes the electronic ground state. Excited states, optical spectra, transport — these need extensions (TDDFT, GW, Bethe–Salpeter) or different machinery entirely.
  • Cost scales steeply. Roughly with the cube of the number of electrons. A few hundred atoms is routine; tens of thousands is a research project on a supercomputer.
  • The XC functional matters. No approximate functional is uniformly best. Reaction barriers, van der Waals forces, strongly correlated systems (transition-metal oxides, f-electron materials) often need special treatment or higher-rung functionals.
  • Dispersion is missing by default. Plain LDA/GGA don't capture van der Waals interactions properly. You add empirical dispersion corrections when they matter (layered materials, molecular crystals).
  • Strong correlation is a blind spot. Systems where electrons are highly localised and entangled (Mott insulators, some magnets) are not well described by standard DFT — that's where DFT+U or beyond-DFT methods come in.

For metallic alloys at equilibrium volumes — the regime this project lives in — standard PBE-DFT is well-calibrated and trusted. It's the reference standard against which faster surrogates (interatomic potentials, neural networks) are validated.
